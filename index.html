An evaluation of tools used in the discovery of antimicrobial natural products

Yuting Tseng
Supervised by Leonid Chindelevitch

Learn More
`

Lay summary

In the past decades, there has been a sharp decline in the discovery of new antimicrobial drugs. Alongside the increasing cases of multiple antibiotic resistance, we are in desperate need of alternative antibiotic therapies. A newly discovered class of antimicrobial peptides, ribosomally synthesised and post-translationally modified peptides (RiPPs), reveals that a large reservoir of potential antimicrobial secondary metabolites in the bacterial genome remains cryptic. The discovery of bioactive RiPPs could have significant implications in medicine and a great amount of effort has been put into uncovering the silent biosynthetic pathways. While traditional identification and characterisation methods require laborious analysis of the metabolomic data, high throughput bioinformatics tools have been developed to accelerate studies on the secondary metabolites.

This project aims to explore and evaluate the RiPP discovery tools currently available by reproducing the results reported in the literature. The three RiPP discovery programmes explored in this analysis represent three different approaches, which are sequence-spectrum matching, precursor peptide identification using machine learning and de novo synthesis of nucleotide sequences. Metabolomic and genomic data extracted from publicly available databases were analysed and results were used for the evaluation of the predictive programmes. It was revealed that the research on the peptidogenomic RiPP identification pipeline suffers from low reproducibility due to data availability and programmatic issues resulting in unexpected errors. Furthermore, the different neural network architectures used in the machine learning predictive tool may have predictive biases toward one or more classes of RiPP. Moreover, the programme considering the de novo synthesis of RiPP failed to execute due to version incompatibility. Finally, several solutions to overcome the limitations and facilitate comprehensive RiPP discovery were suggested.
